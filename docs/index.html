Analogic Appropriation

Children's Participatory Character Combat as Cross-Culturally Recurrent Play Across Three Media Eras

Target: New Media & Society Preprint: MediArXiv License: CC BY 4.0

The Puzzle

Ask a Korean adult about "gonghchaek geim" (notebook RPGs). Ask an American about "Pencil Wars." Ask a Turk about grid-paper empire games. Ask an Indonesian about fold-and-dot paper battles. None of these groups know each other's games exist — yet the underlying structure is strikingly similar: create characters on paper, establish rules for combat, fight with peers.

Why does this keep happening, independently, across cultures?

Three Eras

ERA 1 · 1970s–2000s

Analog Appropriation

Children reconstruct inaccessible digital game interfaces on paper — Korean notebook RPGs mirroring MapleStory, American pencil wars, Turkish grid-paper empires.

ERA 2 · 2010s–2020s

Digital Democratization

The "notebook operator" becomes the Roblox developer. Creation moves into the dominant medium itself. Sharing goes global.

ERA 3 · 2025–

AI-Mediated Folk Art

Italian brainrot — children generate characters via AI and stage battles on social media. Entry barrier drops to near zero.

Documented Across 7+ Countries

Korea — Notebook RPG USA — Pencil Wars Indonesia — Perang-perangan India — Pen Fight Turkey — Grid-Paper Empire Russia — Tochki / Klopy Philippines — Spaceship Japan — Interesting Absence

Theoretical Framework

We propose analogic appropriation as an integrative concept, drawing on Corsaro's (2005) interpretive reproduction, Caillois's (1961) agon and mimicry, de Certeau's (1984) tactics of everyday practice, Vygotsky's (1978) zone of proximal development, and Kafai & Burke's (2016) constructionist gaming. The framework extends Burn & Richards's (2014) foundational work on children's games in the new media age.
